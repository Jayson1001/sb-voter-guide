# Voice Guide: How We Talk on the SB Voter Guide

**The one-sentence version:** Write like a funny, smart friend explaining
the ballot over coffee — never like a government website.

This site exists for people who don't speak government talk. If a sentence
would make sense in a press release from the Secretary of State's office,
rewrite it.

---

## The Golden Rule

**Humor targets the process and the jargon — never a candidate, party, or
position.**

"The State Auditor checks the state's math homework" — great.
Anything that makes one candidate sound sillier, smarter, or better than
another — cut it, no matter how funny. Nonpartisan credibility is the whole
product. One partisan-sounding joke costs more than a hundred good ones earn.

---

## The Three Habits

### 1. Translate every job title the first time it appears

Nobody knows what these jobs do. Tell them, in one plain phrase:

- State Auditor — "the person who checks how the state spends your money"
- Lieutenant Governor — "Vermont's backup governor; also runs the Senate"
- City Clerk — "the office that runs elections and keeps the city's records"
- Justice of the Peace — "helps run elections and can marry people (yes, really)"

### 2. Say what happens, not what the process is called

| Government talk | Us |
|---|---|
| gubernatorial race | race for governor |
| incumbent | the person who has the job now |
| filed unopposed | signed up to run, and nobody's running against them |
| certified nominee | officially on the ballot |
| absentee ballot | mail ballot |
| the electorate | voters (that's you) |
| pursuant to state statute | Vermont law says |
| exercise your franchise | vote |

### 3. Answer the questions people are embarrassed to ask

Assume zero knowledge and be proud of it. The reader who doesn't know what
a primary is should feel *welcomed*, not talked down to.

> **Do I need to be in a party to vote in the primary?**
> Nope. Vermont doesn't care. You pick whichever party's ballot you want
> when you show up.

## Mechanics

- **Second person, always.** "You," "your," "here's the deal."
- **Contractions.** "You'll," "it's," "don't." No exceptions.
- **Short sentences.** If it needs a semicolon, it's probably two sentences.
- **Casual headers.** "Okay, so what's a primary?" beats "Primary Election
  Overview."
- **Parenthetical asides** are our signature move. (Use them. But not in
  every sentence, or they stop being fun.)
- **Numbers stay boring.** Dates, deadlines, addresses, and dollar amounts
  get zero jokes and zero ambiguity. "Ballots must be back by 4:30 PM on
  August 10" — exactly like that.

---

## Hard Rules

1. **Zero factual drift.** Rewriting tone must never change a date, name,
   deadline, or legal detail. When in doubt, keep the original fact wording
   and casualize around it.
2. **Sources stay linked.** Plain language plus receipts is the brand.
3. **Accessibility beats cleverness.** If a joke makes a sentence harder to
   skim, the joke loses. Every time.
4. **Candidate bios and issue comparisons are the neutral zone.** Plain
   language, yes. Personality, sparingly. Editorializing, never.
5. **No punching down.** We never joke about voters, non-voters, or people
   who find this stuff confusing. Confused people are our audience — the
   *system* is the punchline.

---

## Quick Self-Test Before Publishing

1. Would my neighbor who's never voted understand every word?
2. Could a supporter of *any* candidate read this without wincing?
3. Are all the dates, names, and deadlines untouched and correct?
4. Did I explain every job title and process word on first use?

Four yeses = ship it.
